## Post 1

The three options every seed-stage founder reaches for all fail in the same way.

Agency: You're a $15K/month client competing for attention against their $80K/month retainer. You get the junior team. You get slow.

Freelancer: You hire five of them to cover five functions. Now you're the project manager for five separate contractors who don't talk to each other.

Full-time hire: You post the job, interview for six weeks, make an offer, wait 30 days for notice, then spend 60 days onboarding someone into a company that is still figuring out what it is.

By the time any of these work, your window has closed.

The real problem isn't access to talent. It's that early-stage companies need senior-level execution across marketing, engineering, product, and sales simultaneously — not sequentially, not partially, not eventually.

The staffing model that works for a 200-person company was never designed to work for a 12-person startup. Founders keep trying to fit that model anyway because no one has shown them a better option.

That's what I work on.

## Post 3

Most founders hire to solve a problem that isn't a headcount problem.

The content isn't working, so they hire a content manager.
The pipeline is dry, so they hire an SDR.
The product is slipping, so they hire a second engineer.

Six months later, the content manager is waiting on a strategy that doesn't exist. The SDR is sending emails into a positioning vacuum. The second engineer is building features nobody prioritized properly.

The problem was never the person. The problem was the operating model they were hired into.

Hiring before you have a clear process, a clear owner structure, and a clear definition of done is how you spend 3 to 6 months of runway to learn that the real issue was upstream.

The hire accelerates the problem. It doesn't fix it.

What actually needs to happen first: a clear view of what the function is supposed to produce, who is accountable for it, how success is measured, and what inputs the new person will need to execute.

That's not a 90-day onboarding plan. That's a two-week strategy engagement.

One is $300K loaded annual cost plus time to productivity. The other is a fraction of that, and the hire you eventually make lands in something that actually works.

What did your last hire walk into when they started?

## Post 4

A VP of Marketing at a Series B company costs $220K per year before equity.

A VP of Engineering costs $270K.

A strong Head of Product is $200K.

Founders at seed stage know they need this level of judgment in their corner. They also know they cannot afford to hire it. So they do one of two things.

They hire someone cheaper and hope that person grows into the role. Or they do the work themselves and accept that they're spending 30 hours a week executing functions they were never trained for.

Both paths have the same outcome: senior-level execution doesn't happen. The company runs on junior work and founder bandwidth until one of those things breaks.

This is the access gap. And it compounds.

A company with a VP of Marketing on day one gets positioning clarity, a go-to-market framework, and a content engine built correctly from the start. A company without one spends 18 months rebuilding what should have been right from the beginning.

The gap between what well-resourced companies can afford and what early-stage companies can access is not a talent gap. It's a structural one.

The founders who close it early are the ones who figure out how to get senior execution without the senior salary.

## Post 5

Most strategy engagements produce documents nobody acts on.

Not because the thinking was bad. Because the output wasn't built to be used.

Here's the difference.

A weak content deliverable says: "Publish 10 blog posts per month targeting cloud cost optimization topics. Focus on SEO and thought leadership."

A strong one says:

"Week 1, Tuesday: 'How FinOps Teams Are Using Tagging Policies to Cut AWS Spend by 30%' — 1,400 words — targets keyword cluster: AWS cost tagging, cloud tagging strategy, FinOps tagging — CTA: download the tagging checklist — distribute via LinkedIn CEO post + newsletter — repurpose slide 3 from the product deck as a visual."

One is a recommendation. The other is an instruction set.

The test I use: can a competent person execute this deliverable without asking me a single follow-up question?

If the answer is no, the deliverable isn't finished. It's a draft of a strategy, not a strategy.

This is true for content calendars, go-to-market plans, engineering specs, and sales playbooks. Specificity is not a nice-to-have. It is the entire value of the engagement.

A consultant who hands you a direction has done half the job. The other half is the detail work that turns a direction into something you can actually run.

What's the least actionable deliverable you've ever paid for?
