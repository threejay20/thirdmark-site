# Thirdmark Sales Companion Guide

> Read this once before your first call. After that, use it as your reference during every pitch.

---

## Section 1: What You're Selling (Read This First)

Thirdmark is an AI-native agency. That means one account lead, a real human strategist, handles intake, strategy, quality review, and delivery on every engagement. Behind that account lead is a system of 184 specialized AI agents, each built for a specific function: product strategy, engineering, marketing, design, sales, finance, operations. When a client needs a launch plan, a content calendar, a technical spec, or a pitch deck, those agents work in parallel. The account lead reviews everything, applies judgment, and delivers a finished product. The client gets senior-level output across every function, in days instead of months, at a fraction of what a traditional team costs.

What makes this different from everything else you have probably sold before is that there is no handoff chain. There is no junior team working through a project manager working through a strategist working through a creative director. There is one person the client talks to, and behind that person is a production system that runs at a pace no human team can match. You are not selling a retainer with vague deliverables. You are selling a specific output, delivered in a specific timeframe, at a price point that makes the comparison to any alternative look obvious. The conversation is not about convincing someone that AI is useful. It is about showing someone that the output is already good and the economics already work.

## Section 3: Qualifying a Prospect (Before the Deck Opens)

Ask these five questions before you open any screen share or start the pitch. The point is to know what tier fits, whether this is a real opportunity, and whether you are talking to the right person. Listen carefully. The words matter less than the pattern underneath them.

---

**Question 1: "What are you working on right now, and what is the biggest thing slowing you down?"**

What to listen for (good fit signal): A specific execution bottleneck. "We need to get our content going but we have nobody to do it." "We are launching in six weeks and we do not have our messaging figured out." "We have a product but no outbound." These are real problems with real urgency.

What to listen for (not a fit or wrong tier): Vague strategic exploration with no time pressure. "We are thinking about what our marketing strategy should look like eventually." "We want to figure out our positioning before we do anything." If there is no execution problem, there is no reason to buy. This person may need a consultant, not an agency.

---

**Question 2: "Do you have anyone in-house handling this right now, or are you running without that function?"**

What to listen for (good fit signal): No one in-house, or a founder doing it badly on the side, or a single generalist who is stretched too thin. These are the exact situations Thirdmark fills. The absence of a team is the sales condition, not the objection.

What to listen for (not a fit signal): "We have a full marketing team but we are looking for extra capacity." This becomes a vendor management conversation and procurement may be involved. Thirdmark works best as the primary execution layer, not as overflow capacity for a team that already has process and politics around it.

---

**Question 3: "What stage is the company at, and how many people are on the team?"**

What to listen for (good fit signal): Pre-seed through Series A, 3 to 100 people. The sweet spot is a team small enough that everyone is wearing multiple hats but funded enough to have some budget. Seed-stage founders who just closed a round and need to start executing fast are ideal.

What to listen for (not a fit signal): Enterprise or late-stage company. These organizations have procurement processes, legal review cycles, preferred vendor lists, and committee approvals. Thirdmark can serve them, but the sales cycle is fundamentally different and the entry point is usually a pilot through a specific team, not a company-wide contract. If they are describing RFPs or security reviews upfront, note it and adjust expectations accordingly.

---

**Question 4: "Have you worked with an agency or hired contractors for this kind of work before? How did that go?"**

What to listen for (good fit signal): A bad experience with a traditional agency. Slow delivery, junior team doing the work, high cost for mediocre output, vague deliverables, long ramp time. Every one of those frustrations is a direct contrast point with Thirdmark. Listen for the specific pain because you will use it in the pitch.

What to listen for (not a fit or harder sale signal): "We tried to build it in-house and we prefer that model." This person wants to own the capability, not outsource it. You can still sell to them but the conversation is different. The question becomes what they cannot staff quickly enough, and where Thirdmark fills the gap while they build.

---

**Question 5: "Is budget something you have figured out, or is that still TBD?"**

What to listen for (good fit signal): Any acknowledgment that budget exists, even if it is not defined. "We have some runway for this." "I have a small marketing budget." "We are planning to spend somewhere in the $5K to $10K range." These are green lights. You do not need them to name a number. You need to know that money is not the gating factor.

What to listen for (not a fit signal): "We are not planning to spend anything right now, we are just exploring options." This is a tire-kicker. Thirdmark does not have a free tier. If there is no budget signal at all and no urgency, this is not a qualified opportunity. Be direct about it: "I want to make sure we are not wasting your time. Our entry point is $1,500 for a one-time sprint. Is that in range for you at all?" Their answer tells you everything.

## Section 4: Slide-by-Slide Talk Track

### Slide 1: Cover

**What to say:**
"Let me walk you through how Thirdmark works and show you what an engagement actually looks like. Before I get into the slides, I want to make sure the conversation is relevant to what you are dealing with right now, so feel free to stop me at any point."

**Question to ask:**
"Is there a specific area you are most focused on today, or do you want to see the full picture first?"

**What to watch for:**
If they immediately name a specific function like marketing, engineering, or product, that is your cue to weight that section of the pitch more heavily. If they say "the full picture," they are still in discovery mode. Both are fine. Note which it is.

---

### Slide 2: The Problem

**What to say:**
"Most early-stage teams are trying to execute across six or seven different functions at the same time but they can only staff one or two of them. So things fall behind. Marketing is slow because the founder is doing it part-time. The technical spec is not written because there is no architect on the team. The pitch deck looks like it was made in a weekend because it was. The work is getting done, but not at the level that moves the company forward."

**Question to ask:**
"Where does that show up most for your team right now? What is the function that is either not happening or happening at a level that is not good enough?"

**What to watch for:**
This is the most important question in the deck. If they answer immediately and specifically, the deal is real. If they get vague or say "kind of everything," ask a follow-up: "If you had to pick one thing to fix in the next 30 days, what would it be?" You want a specific answer before you move forward.

---

### Slide 3: Why Nothing Works

**What to say:**
"The typical solutions all have the same problem. A traditional agency takes four to six weeks to start and charges $8K to $20K a month for work that is mostly done by account coordinators and junior staff. Hiring specialists takes two to four months and costs $22K to $43K a month in fully-loaded salary. Freelancers are faster and cheaper but they are hard to coordinate, quality is inconsistent, and managing five freelancers is a part-time job on its own."

**Question to ask:**
"Have you tried any of those routes? What was the experience like?"

**What to watch for:**
If they had a bad agency or hiring experience, do not rush past it. Let them tell the story. The frustration they describe is the exact contrast you will close on. If they have not tried any of these, ask what has been stopping them. The answer is usually one of two things: cost or time, and Thirdmark addresses both.

### Slide 4: How Thirdmark Works

**What to say:**
"Thirdmark solves this with a different architecture. One account lead handles strategy, intake, and quality review. Behind that account lead is a system of 184 specialized AI agents, each one purpose-built for a specific function. When you need a content calendar and a homepage rewrite and a technical architecture doc at the same time, those workstreams run in parallel. The account lead reviews everything before it reaches you. You get the output, not the process."

**Question to ask:**
"Does that model make sense? Do you have any questions about how the account lead and the agent system actually work together?"

**What to watch for:**
The most common question here is about quality control. "How do I know the output is good?" Do not wait for them to ask it. Pre-empt it: "The account lead is the quality filter. Nothing goes to the client without a human reviewing it against a defined standard. We score everything before delivery." If they seem skeptical about AI output, that is handled in the objection section. Note it and address it directly.

## Section 5: Objection Handling

### Objection 1: "How do I know the quality is good?"

The quality question is fair and it deserves a direct answer. Every deliverable at Thirdmark goes through a defined QA process before it reaches the client, scored against a standard that requires a 94 out of 100 to pass. The account lead reviews everything personally. If something does not meet that standard, it does not ship. The best way to see what quality looks like in practice is to look at actual output, and I can share examples from engagements similar to yours right now.

Would it help to walk through a sample deliverable so you can judge the quality yourself before we talk about next steps?

---

### Objection 2: "I've tried AI tools before and the output was generic."

That is a real and fair concern. Using a general-purpose AI tool and getting a finished, client-ready deliverable are not the same thing. The tools you tried were built to generate content. Thirdmark is built to deliver specific business outcomes, with agents that are purpose-built for each function and an account lead who reviews and refines the output against the context of your actual business. The CloudHarbor case is a good example: the output was specific enough that the client used it directly. Generic output would not have passed our QA standard.

What was the specific thing you tried to use AI for where the output did not work?

---

### Objection 3: "You're one person. What if you're not available?"

The account lead model is intentional because it gives clients one point of contact and direct access to the person making decisions. On the production side, the agent system runs continuously and does not depend on any single person's availability. If there is ever an issue with availability, the scope and delivery timeline are committed in writing at the start of every engagement, so you are not dependent on best-effort estimates. The answer is also simply: we do not overschedule. Each account lead carries a defined client load and does not take on more than can be delivered at the standard we commit to.

Is there a specific scenario you are worried about? I want to make sure we address it directly.

---

### Objection 4: "I need to think about it."

That is completely reasonable and I do not want to push you toward something before you are ready. It helps me to understand what you are still weighing so I can give you whatever you need to make a clear decision. Usually when someone needs to think about it, they are either still comparing options, waiting on a budget decision, or not fully convinced on one specific point. Which of those is closest to where you are?

---

### Objection 5: "That's more than I was expecting to spend."

I hear that. The Sprint starts at $1,500 for a single deliverable and there is no commitment beyond that, so the entry point is actually quite low relative to what you get. The comparison that usually shifts this conversation is the alternative cost: the same scope from a freelancer or a traditional agency is going to cost two to four times as much and take three to four times as long. If budget is genuinely a constraint right now, the Sprint is designed exactly for that situation. You get real output, you see the quality, and you decide if it is worth continuing.

What were you expecting to spend, and what specific deliverable were you hoping to get for that?

---

### Objection 6: "We're not ready yet."

Tell me more about what "not ready" means for you. In most cases when I hear that, the company is actually in exactly the right stage for Thirdmark because the value is highest when you are moving fast and do not have a full team yet. "Not ready" sometimes means "we do not have the strategy figured out," which is actually something we can deliver. Sometimes it means "we are waiting until after the fundraise," which is worth talking through because a lot of the output that helps fundraises is something we produce. What would have to change for you to feel ready?

---

### Objection 7: "Can you just do a small test first?"

Yes, and that is exactly what the AI Sprint is designed for. It is a one-time engagement, one clear deliverable, three to seven business days, no commitment. You see the output before you decide anything about an ongoing relationship. A lot of clients start with a Sprint and convert to a Retainer after they see the quality. What would be the most useful single deliverable for you right now so we can scope a Sprint that actually proves something meaningful to your team?

## Section 6: Tier Matching Guide

### Signals That Point to AI Sprint

1. "I just need one thing done." The prospect can name a specific deliverable: a pitch deck, a content calendar, a homepage rewrite, a technical spec.
2. No recurring need yet. They are pre-launch or very early and do not have ongoing execution needs.
3. Budget caution. They want to see the quality before committing to a monthly engagement.
4. Fast one-time deadline. "I have a board meeting in two weeks" or "we are launching in 10 days."

### Signals That Point to AI Retainer

1. "We need to keep the output going every month." They describe ongoing needs: regular content, continuous product iteration, monthly reporting.
2. Seed-stage with a small team running the company. Two to six people doing everything. No dedicated function leads.
3. Budget is available but not unlimited. They can do $5K to $15K a month but do not need everything at once.
4. Single primary function with depth. They need marketing, or they need sales enablement, but not necessarily five functions in parallel.

### Signals That Point to AI Studio

1. "We need to scale across everything." They are describing work in multiple functions simultaneously: hiring support, product launches, marketing campaigns, sales pipeline building.
2. Series A or later, growing fast, and behind on execution. The company is ahead of its operational capability.
3. Budget is not the primary concern. Speed and coverage are. They can invest $15K to $35K a month to move faster.
4. Multiple stakeholders with different needs. The marketing lead needs content, the CTO needs architecture docs, the CEO needs board materials. All at once.

### How to Recommend a Tier

When you are ready to recommend, say it directly and give a reason:

"Based on what you described, I think the AI Sprint is the right starting point. You need one specific thing done fast, and the Sprint is exactly that: one deliverable, delivered in days, no commitment. Let me scope it out for you so you can see exactly what you are getting."

"What you are describing sounds like an AI Retainer situation. You have ongoing needs across marketing and sales enablement, no one in-house to handle it, and a timeline that is already behind. The Retainer gives you a defined set of deliverables each month for a fixed investment. Let me show you what that scope would look like for your specific situation."

"Honestly, everything you are describing points to AI Studio. You need product, marketing, and sales enablement running simultaneously, and you need it all fast. Studio is the full-stack model where we are running multiple workstreams in parallel every month. Let me walk you through what that looks like so you can see if the investment makes sense."

## Section 9: Common Scenarios

### Scenario A: Prospect Is Interested but Keeps Saying "Not Yet"

**What is actually happening:**
"Not yet" almost always means one of three things: they have not yet convinced their internal decision partner, the budget is not confirmed, or they are genuinely interested but facing something more urgent. Rarely does "not yet" mean "never." What it does mean is that they are not in pain acutely enough to act right now.

**What to say on the call:**
"I hear you. Can I ask what would need to change for this to move from not yet to yes? Is it timing, budget, something you need to see first, or something else?" Then stop talking and let them answer. Their answer tells you exactly what to do next.

**What to send:**
A one-page scope document with a defined expiration date. "This scope is based on our conversation and is held through [date 10 days out]. After that I will need to reassess based on capacity." Scarcity without pressure. It is honest because you do have capacity constraints.

**Follow-up cadence:**
Day 1 after the call: Send the scope and the summary note.
Day 4: Brief check-in. "Did you get a chance to look at the scope? Any questions?"
Day 10: Final follow-up before the scope expires. "Wanted to give you a heads-up that I am holding the scope through [date]. Happy to adjust the scope or timeline if that helps. Let me know either way."
After Day 10: If no response, move them to a 30-day reactivation. Send one message in 30 days: "Checking back in. Is this still on your radar?"

Do not chase beyond four touches. Four meaningful contacts is the limit. After that, let them come to you.

---

### Scenario B: Prospect Asks for a Custom Scope Outside the Three Tiers

**What is actually happening:**
This is usually a good sign. It means they are engaged enough to think about specifics. The three tiers are pricing anchors, not rigid cages. Almost every engagement ends up as some variation of the defined tiers.

**What to say:**
"The tiers are a starting point for scoping. Everything we do is custom inside those frameworks. Tell me more about what you are trying to accomplish and I will scope it specifically. The investment will fall somewhere in one of those three ranges depending on volume and complexity, but the deliverables will be exactly what you need."

**When to handle yourself:**
If the ask is a recognizable deliverable or combination of deliverables that fits within the scope of any existing tier, scope it yourself. Use the tier that best matches on price and volume.

**When to escalate:**
If the prospect is describing something genuinely outside the six delivery areas, requires external data access, involves compliance or regulated industries, or implies a staffing commitment rather than a deliverable commitment, flag it before you quote. Say: "Let me run this by the account lead to make sure I am scoping it accurately. I will come back to you within 24 hours with a specific scope and investment." Then do exactly that.

---

### Scenario C: Prospect Had a Bad Experience With an Agency Before

**What is actually happening:**
This is extremely common and it is not a reason the deal will not close. In most cases the bad experience was with a traditional agency model: slow ramp, junior staff doing the work, vague deliverables, and lots of money for mediocre output. That experience is actually your best setup for the Thirdmark pitch.

**The reframe:**
Do not argue with their experience. Validate it directly. "That is the norm with traditional agencies and it is exactly why we built Thirdmark differently. The model you described, where you pay senior agency rates and get junior execution, is built into their business model. They need that margin to survive. Ours works differently because the production layer is not a team of junior writers. It is a system of specialized agents, and the output gets reviewed against a defined quality standard before it ever reaches you."

**What to say:**
"Tell me what went wrong specifically. Was it the quality, the timeline, the communication, or the value relative to cost?" Let them talk. Then map each specific frustration to a specific contrast: slow timeline becomes 72-hour start and three to seven day delivery; junior quality becomes QA scoring and account lead review; vague deliverables becomes specific scope documents before every engagement; poor communication becomes single account lead contact.

**How to move them from skeptical to curious:**
Offer them a look at an actual deliverable before they commit to anything. "I do not want you to take my word for it. Let me share a sample from a recent engagement similar to yours so you can see the output quality directly." Real output answers the skepticism better than any explanation. Once they see the work, the conversation shifts.
